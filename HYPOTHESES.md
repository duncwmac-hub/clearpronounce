### ClearPronounce – Working Hypotheses

This file lists explicit hypotheses about pronunciation, UX, and learning. Each hypothesis should eventually have a simple test attached and a status.

Status legend:  
- **OPEN** – not tested yet  
- **TESTING** – test in progress or designed  
- **SUPPORTED** – evidence so far supports it  
- **REJECTED** – evidence suggests it’s wrong or misleading for our users  

---

#### H1 – /ʌ/ (â) is the best first “new sound” to teach Polish learners

- **Statement (ELF + Oxford3000 informed)**  
  Teaching `/ʌ/` (represented as `â`) as a distinct English vowel unlocks **more high‑value words** for Polish learners than teaching any other single new sound first (e.g. `/θ/` or `/ð/`), and learners find the `â` symbol intuitive enough to use.

- **Why we think this**  
  - `/ʌ/` appears in many very frequent words (*but, some, come, must, number, under, another, young, country, love, up…*).  
  - Polish has no direct equivalent, so it’s a genuine gap.  
  - Fixing this early should noticeably improve intelligibility and reduce “Polish accent confusion”.

- **Planned test (100‑learner respelling test)**  
  - Show Polish learners pairs of words and respellings involving `/ʌ/` vs nearby vowels (e.g. *but–bat, some–same, cut–cat*).  
  - Ask:
    - “Czy ten zapis (â) pomaga Ci usłyszeć/wyprodukować inny dźwięk niż polskie a/e?”  
    - Simple discrimination tasks (choose the word that matches the audio, with respellings visible).  
  - Track:
    - How often learners correctly distinguish `/ʌ/` from /a/ or /æ/ when guided by `â`.  
    - Whether they report `â` as “clear and helpful” vs “weird/confusing”.

- **Status:** OPEN

#### H2 – For ELF clarity, pre‑stress schwa should be over‑pronounced in respelling

- **Statement**  
  For words like *about, again, ago*, a respelling that uses a full vowel in the first syllable (e.g. `aBAUT`, `aGEN`, `aGOŁ`) is **more helpful for Polish learners’ production and intelligibility** than a strictly schwa‑based respelling (e.g. `əBAUT`), while still letting teachers see the true IPA `/ə/`.

- **Why we think this**  
  - ELF / Lingua Franca Core research treats weak forms and heavy schwa reduction as **non‑core**, and sometimes **harmful** to NNS–NNS intelligibility.  
  - Polish learners are more likely to succeed with a slightly over‑pronounced but stable vowel than with fully reduced forms, especially early on.

- **Planned test**  
  - In the learner test, include items like *about, again, ago, above, around* with two respelling variants:
    - “Careful/Global” (e.g. `aBAUT`),  
    - “Reduced/Native‑like” (e.g. `əBAUT`).  
  - Ask which variant:
    - Looks clearer,  
    - Feels easier to say,  
    - Feels closer to how they actually say it now.  
  - Optionally, simple recording or self‑judgement tasks to see if `a…` respellings get them closer to target than `ə…` respellings.

- **Status:** OPEN

#### H3 – A tiny core set of “unlock sounds” is enough for useful teacher feedback

- **Statement**  
  For Polish learners, restricting the main “sounds your class is OK with” checklist to just `/θ, ð, ŋ, ʌ, ə` provides **enough signal** for teachers to plan pronunciation work, without needing a larger list of sounds (e.g. `/aɪ, aʊ, oʊ, ʊ, æ, ɝ/`).

- **Why we think this**  
  - Many other English sounds have close Polish equivalents or can be approximated without special symbols in early stages.  
  - A long list of checkboxes is cognitively heavy and discouraging for tired teachers.  
  - Focusing on 4–5 truly non‑Polish sounds keeps the UI simple and matches ELF priorities (segmental clarity, a few key contrasts).

- **Planned test**  
  - Run teacher interviews / short surveys after using the tool on real texts:
    - “Czy ta mała lista problematycznych dźwięków (th, â, schwa, ng) jest dla Ciebie wystarczająco informacyjna?”  
    - “Czy brak innych dźwięków (np. dyftongów) przeszkadza Ci w planowaniu lekcji?”  
  - Observe whether teachers spontaneously ask for more sounds in the list or are relieved that it’s small.

- **Status:** OPEN

#### H4 – Teachers will accept non‑native‑like but consistent respelling if it saves prep time

- **Statement**  
  Teachers are willing to accept a respelling system that is not a perfect match to any traditional dictionary or phonetics textbook, as long as it is **internally consistent**, clearly explained, and **saves them noticeable prep time** for pronunciation work.

- **Why we think this**  
  - Many practising teachers are pragmatists; they already invent ad‑hoc respellings on the board.  
  - A consistent, Polish‑aware system that “just appears” for any text may be a net win, even if it offends purist phonetics.

- **Planned test**  
  - Show ClearPronounce to 10–20 Polish EFL teachers using *their* lesson texts.  
  - Ask:
    - “Czy ten zapis wymowy jest wystarczająco jasny, żeby użyć go jutro na lekcji?”  
    - “Co byś zmienił(a), żeby był bardziej użyteczny?”  
  - Measure:
    - How many say they would actually use it in lesson prep.  
    - What objections are about **consistency/clarity** vs pure “it’s not canonical phonetics”.

- **Status:** OPEN
